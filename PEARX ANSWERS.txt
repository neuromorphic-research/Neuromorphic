The Problem 🤔
What are you building, and why?
*
We are building easy-to-integrate camera systems that run vision-language models in real time, allowing robots to understand what they see. Instead of treating cameras as passive sensors, we turn them into semantic perception modules that can identify objects, interpret scenes, and provide actionable context directly to robotic systems.


We are building this because robotics still struggles with perception in unstructured real-world environments. Traditional vision systems are often brittle, narrow, and expensive to adapt for each use case. By making real-time semantic understanding plug-and-play, we help robotics companies deploy smarter systems faster, with less integration overhead and far more flexibility.


What unique insight do you have into this problem?
*
The core insight is that robot autonomy is often limited not by motion or control, but by the lack of deployable semantic understanding under real-world compute and integration constraints. We know this because we have shipped and deployed these systems ourselves, and today there is still no plug-and-play product that works reliably enough in production. As a result, many robots still fall back to teleoperation or heavy human oversight, and we are building the missing perception layer to close that gap.

Competition and Market 🏇
Who are your competitors? How do you differentiate from them?
*
Our closest alternatives are depth and edge-AI camera platforms like Stereolabs ZED, RealSense, and Luxonis OAK. Those products are built around stereo/depth perception, spatial awareness, or general on-device vision inference, but robotics teams still have to build and maintain their own semantic understanding stack on top of them.


We differentiate by making semantic understanding itself the product: a plug-and-play camera module that runs real-time VLM perception for robots under real deployment compute constraints. Instead of giving teams raw visual data plus integration work, we give them actionable context directly, with far less perception engineering overhead
How big is the market opportunity? Provide a bottoms-up calculation of how you estimated the market size.
*
Our bottom-up market estimate starts with annual robot deployments: roughly 742k new robots are deployed per year globally across industrial and professional service categories. We assume semantic perception is required for the subset operating in dynamic or unstructured environments—about 35% of industrial robots and 60% of service/mobile robots—yielding ~309k addressable robots annually. At an average of 1.6 perception modules per robot, that corresponds to ~495k modules per year; with a blended ASP of ~$2.5k per module plus ~$1.2k in annual software/support, this represents an initial annual revenue opportunity of roughly $1.8B. Beyond this base market, we expect to expand adoption further by lowering integration cost, deployment time, and model-tuning overhead, which makes semantic perception viable for a broader set of robotics OEMs and applications.

Please share an example of a time you tackled a problem in a novel way and achieved something truly impressive.
*
Compressing neural networks usually means forcing a small "student" model to perfectly mimic the internal activations of a massive "teacher" model, which inevitably degrades performance. I realized we were optimizing the wrong thing: a student doesn't need to copy everything, just the features that actually impact the final output. I developed a framework around this called NSA-Net. Instead of enforcing an exact match, we mathematically projected the mismatch between the models directly into the "null space" of the teacher's layers. Any discrepancies were safely absorbed where they couldn't affect the network's flow, forcing the student to strictly learn task-relevant directions. It decreased parameter counts by up to 90% and computation by 80%. Because this null-space projection acted as a powerful regularizer, our highly compressed models actually outperformed their massive teachers on benchmarks like CIFAR-10, and our paper was published at APSIPA ASC 2025. The core insight was weaponizing a network's mathematical blind spots to selectively filter out noise rather than fighting to perfectly replicate it.
